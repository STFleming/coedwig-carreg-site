Barney McGrue Backstory

As a young woodgrue, I spent long stretches of time alone. Our kind drift in and out of each other’s company, and I learned early not to expect anyone to stay long, even my parents. One season the treetop den fell silent and remained that way for some days. Hunger pulled at me until I finally climbed down to the forest floor and ate whatever I found lying there. Woodgrue are not fussy, and our curiosity often gets the better of us.

After days of wandering like this, I found a cluster of pale fungus growing in a damp fold of moss. It looked soft enough to bite, so I did. The taste spread through me in a warm rush, and the forest folded open. The light shifted and the shadows breathed. The sounds of the forest changed, the leaves whispered with a strange clarity, and I saw shapes that felt familiar even though I could not fully grasp them. In that warped brightness, two figures stepped out of the blur. They reached toward me as though they recognised me in a moment of warmth that didn’t seem to end. After who knows how long, my awareness of the forest snapped back, brittle and cold. My head throbbed and I opened my eyes to find myself among the fungus. My mouth was dry and bitter tasting. As my senses awoke, my vision focused on something pale beneath the leaves. It was my father’s bone whistle. The sight of it made the air feel thin. I lifted myself heavily, turning toward the patch of moss where I had woken. It heaved slightly, as if it breathed. When I brushed the moss aside, the ground opened into hollows where ribs had collapsed inward. Threads of fungus curled through them, bright and tender, feeding on what remained. My father’s face had fallen in on itself. His hands were gone. The fungus that had fed me shone softly on the ruin, as though proud of its feast.

I did not call out. The forest around me held its breath, and I held mine with it. A deep understanding settled on me, as I began to see the wood differently. Everything living rose from something dead, the forest breathing with cycles of decay and rebirth. On that day, I had shared the secrets of that cycle and communed with the memory of my father. I began to understand the Wood differently, and my place in it. The forest fed, and the flora remembered. When we consume, we remember with them. I do not pretend to know why my father’s fungi spoke more clearly than others, but I know what I saw. I know what I tasted. I know what I found in the moss that day.

That first brightness stayed in my mind like a bruise. Over time I stopped trying to forget it. Certain fungi carried a scent or a colour that reminded me of it, and whenever I found one, I tasted it. Some brought nothing. Some brought fragments. None brought my parents back, but a few opened the world a little, just for a moment, in that same impossible way. Now I gather fungi the way others gather bones. I keep the ones that whisper. I trade the ones that hum with memory. The Wood is full of the dead, tangled in its roots and dripping through its soil, and some of them tell me their stories for me to pass to others in the wood.

I searched for my mother for a long time after that, but the Wood kept its secrets. If she returned to it, it swallowed her more neatly than it swallowed him.
